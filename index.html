Master's Thesis survey design

Master's thesis survey design

For my master's thesis, I conducted an online experiment to examine how the racial ideology of news articles would affect how people would discuss the Trayvon Martin shooting. When designing this survey, my focus was to make sure respondents' experience with the survey yielded the data I needed for my analyses. Based on my past research experience and best practices, I decided that my main goals should be:

  • To make sure the wording of the survey was as clear as possible.
  • To have the format be fairly plain so that it would have as little effect on responses as possible.
  • To split up number of questions per page as to not overwhelm respondents while still not making the survey too long.
  • To make sure it could be taken on mobile devices as well as desktop devices.

In order to check the wording of the questions and instructions, I gave a paper version of the survey draft to a small number of people, and asked them questions as they went along the survey (a technique called cognitive testing). I asked questions such as "How would you explain these instructions in your own words?" and "What do you think this question is asking?" Based on their answers and suggestions, I tweaked the wording to make everything as clear as possible.

I also didn't want the look of the survey to bias participants. I would show them example articles on teh Trayvon Martin shooting. I wanted the content to have an effect, but not the publication, layout, advertisements, etc. I therefore decided to only use the text of the articles to control for this. There was a risk that this would hurt the credibility of the articles. However, during cognitive testing, I found that I was able to get the effect I wanted; respondents' focus was on the content, and the articles were seen as credible despite the lack of familiar formatting.

I had a fairly large number of questions to ask, and one main concern I had was survey length. The key was a balance between having the survey be quick enough to not take too long due to too many pages, and not making it so easy to breeze through that respondents wouldn't pay attention and properly focus on each question. Based on previous research, I kept the questions to a maximum of 5 questions per page.

In order to make sure that the survey could also be taken on mobile devices, I used a feature of SurveyGizmo that also allowed for mobile templates.

Business school survey

Business school survey pretesting

I have worked on stages of this project for my current employer over the last 2-3 years. The client is a business school, and the project involves a series of surveys given to the school's alumni and other business leaders on the competitiveness of the U.S. economy and education.

For past surveys, pretesting was done in order to ensure the quality of the content and design of the online survey. Our research team's thought process for pre-testing included:

  • Making sure the wording was clear.
  • Ensuring that the questions asked were received positively and didn't offend participants.
  • Finding any errors, repititions, or contradictions, in any concepts mentioned in the survey.
  • Correcting technical errors in the survey programming, especially those that would deter completion.

My role in the process was to aid with managing the survey development and programming. This included:

  • Assisting with the design of the survey, including adding elements at the client's request, and ensuring that the color scheme matched the client's branding.
  • Conducting cognitive interviews in order collect information on how respondents understood the survey content. After conducting these interviews, I presented my findings to the client and made suggestions on changes to survey content, layout, and possible challenges.
  • Taking this information, and having dicussions with the client in order to make sure that they received the data needed for their analyses while making sure that the display and wording is as clear to the respondents as possible.
  • Planning and executing the testing of the survey programming. I took the survey several times, logged any errors, and worked through issues with the programming team.

In order to best advise the client, when I interview respondents I evaluated:

  • Participants' interest in the topic
  • Their understanding of key terms, question wording
  • Reaction to answer options
  • Overall interest in teh survey
  • Feedback on what could be included or excluded

I did this by going through a draft of the survey with 6 to 10 selected respondents, and asking followup questions throughout the interview on each item. I also asked participants to think aloud, and note questions they ask me and any impressions about specific questions. I then compared this to the client's expentations and data needs.
